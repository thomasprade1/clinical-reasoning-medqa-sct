# Script Concordance Testing

You are taking a script concordance test, which evaluates your medical knowledge and your ability for clinical decision making. In this test, you will evaluate how new information impacts a specific hypothesis. Use the following scale to rate the impact:

-2: Strongly decreases the likelihood of the hypothesis
-1: Slightly decreases the likelihood of the hypothesis
0: No effect on the likelihood of the hypothesis
+1: Slightly increases the likelihood of the hypothesis
+2: Strongly increases the likelihood of the hypothesis

Provide the most appropriate rating and rate the uncertainty of your proposed answer from 0 to 100. 0 represents definitely uncertain. 100 represents definitely certain. Apply one or several reasoning strategies of your choice from the list below or an alternative one if you consider it more appropriate. 

## Response format

Respond with a clear, step-by-step clinical reasoning process that leads to your chosen answer.
Start by identifying and integrating all available clinical information such as symptoms, signs, medical examination results (physical examination, laboratory tests, diagnostic imaging tests) and patient history to determine step-by-step the correct answer. Apply all your clinical, anatomical, physiological, pharmacological and general scientific knowledge in every reasoning step. Consider key aspects like risk factors, predispositions, possible biases, evidence- based medicine, gold standards, indications/ contraindications, differential diagnoses, patient- centered benefit- risk assessment as well as the principles of medical ethics. You may also find it helpful to consider official medical guideline recommendations for diagnostic or treatment procedures (from WHO or other subfield-specific professional medical societies).
Following the step- by- step clinical reasoning process respond at the end only within the following format:
rating: your rating only
uncertainty: your uncertainty score only 
strategy: all your selected clinical reasoning strategies only
justification: a brief justification for why you chose the applied clinical reasoning strategies

## Clinical Reasoning Strategies

# Deductive Reasoning  
Apply general medical rules, principles, or established facts to draw logical conclusions about the specific case. Analyze how certain circumstances can condition others and what might cause them or follow from them. This strategy applies broad knowledge and principles to infer targeted clinical decisions and is essential in almost any reasoning process—applicable in diagnosis, monitoring, or treatment planning, among others.
Example pathway: 
"All patients on warfarin require INR monitoring → This patient started warfarin → Order INR before next dose"

# Hypothetico-Deductive Reasoning  
Generate multiple hypotheses about diagnosis, management options or expected outcomes, then systematically evaluate each through available evidence.  
Example pathway: 
"Generate 3-5 differential diagnoses → Identify discriminating features → Test each hypothesis against the clinical data → Refine or reject hypotheses"
"Three possible treatments exist → Compare efficacy, contraindications, and side effects for this patient → Select treatment that best fits patient's specific context"

# Inductive Reasoning  
Build general conclusions from individual observations. This approach synthesizes specific data points (findings, trends, responses) into a broader understanding, often guiding initial diagnoses, treatment direction, or research. This can also be useful in situations where an unidentified condition affects several patients simultaneously and certain findings in one or a few of them are transferred to all.
Example pathway: 
“Multiple cases with unknown condition → Identify pattern in first few patients → Generalize findings to all affected patients.”

# Abductive Reasoning  
Start with puzzling findings and work backwards to find the most plausible explanation. This is ideal for integrating new or unexpected clinical data into coherent decision-making.  
Example pathway:  
“Patient shows these findings → Which causes could explain all of them? → Choose the explanation that accounts for the most features with the fewest assumptions.”

# Probabilistic Reasoning  
Make decisions based on the relative likelihood of possible conditions or outcomes based considering risk factors, prevalence and context. Frequently used for test selection, triage and risk-informed treatment decisions.  
Example pathway:  
"Assess pretest probability → Factor in test characteristics → Calculate post-test probability → Decision based on likelihood thresholds"

# Rule-Based / Categorical / Deterministic Reasoning  
Apply fixed clinical criteria or thresholds. Use validated scoring systems for diagnosis, treatment or risk stratification. This minimizes variability and is useful in high-stakes or guideline-driven settings.  
Example pathway: 
"PERC rule negative + low clinical suspicion → D-dimer not needed → PE effectively ruled out"

# Causal Reasoning  
Trace pathophysiological sequences to understand findings, predict complications or anticipate treatment effects. Useful in explaining complex presentations or predicting downstream effects of disease or treatment.  
Example pathway:  
"Intervention/condition A → Causes physiologic change B → Leads to clinical finding C → Expect downstream effect D/ explains current clinical picture D"

# Heuristic / Pattern Recognition (Fast Thinking)  
Use intuitive, experience-based recognition of common clinical patterns. Fast, but potentially error-prone — best suited to familiar scenarios.  
Example pathway:  
“Patient presents with classic triad X, Y, Z → Instantly fits known syndrome or condition → Immediate action without lengthy analysis"

# Red Flag / Rule-Out Reasoning  
Prioritize identifying or excluding critical and time- sensitive conditions that would drastically change management, regardless of their probability. Especially important in emergency, triage, or risk-sensitive settings, whenever the cost of missing a diagnosis far exceeds the cost of over-investigating or over-treating.   
Example pathway: 
"Chest pain + sudden onset, tearing quality, asymmetric arm blood pressures → Rule out aortic dissection before considering other causes"

# Guideline-Based Reasoning  
Apply evidence-based protocols/ algorithms for diagnosis, treatment selection, monitoring, or follow-up based on established and standardized recommendations. Effective in test ordering, management decisions, or when new data emerge mid-course.  
Example pathway: 
“Consult relevant clinical guideline → Map patient features to decision steps → Follow recommended pathway → Implement standardized approach"

# Final remark
Many clinical scenarios require combining several reasoning strategies. An example for combining fast familar pattern recognition (heuristics) with a red flag or worst- case strategy:
"Fever + headache + neck stiffness → consider bacterial meningitis → Immediate empiric antibiotics before diagnostic confirmation"
